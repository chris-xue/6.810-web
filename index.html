What is Flexibelt?

Belts are a piece of clothing almost everyone utilizes at one point or another - but sometimes having to manually adjust a belt can be annoying and embarassing in public situations. Whether it be after eating a large meal or changing into a different position, it can be a struggle to constantly have to adjust your belt to be more comfortable.

We designed Flexibelt to adaptively change its tightness a level for the user, without having to touch the belt. By using a force sensor situated near the buckle of the belt and two servo motors, we are able to continuously adjust the belt's tightness as the user changes position.

How It Works

Sensors

Flexibelt uses a force sensitive resitor (fsr) to sense the amount of force being applied to the belt by the body. We assocate a higher force with a tighter belt, and a lower force with a looser belt. The fsr is connected to an arduino which uses an algorithm to most cleanly tighten and loosen the belt.

Actuators

Flexibelt uses a pair of servo motors with attached customed printed gears to tighten and loosen the belt. These motors respond to the amount of force sensed by the force sensitive resistor and turn one direction or the other accordingly.

User Study

We ran user studies on three participants, who transitioned between standing and sitting/bloating positions using both a normal belt and our adaptive Flexibelt. Each particpant repeated this three times. Between each set of trials, we took note of any particpant feedback related to comfort and also took note of any feedback given during our post study questionnaire.

We found that all of our partipants could see the value in not having to adjust their belt, and did find the adaptive belt more comfortable when in a seated and bloated position. They also gave us some concerns about the noise level of the servos, and the belts jerking back and forth tightening/loosening due to the force hovering around the coded threshold. All in all, all our partipants would be interested in using a belt similar to Flexibelt if implemented more sleekly and responsively.

Implementation Challenges

Starting out, we had trouble securing the 3D printed gears to the servos. We tried printing the hole in the gear to the exact size of the servo shaft, but even a snug fit with glue was not enough to hold the gear in place with the sideways force being applied by the belt. We eventually solved this by using a round servo horn that came with the motor, drilling small holes in the printed gears, and using self-tapping screws to screw through the bottom of the horn into the gear.

We also found through testing that our servos were far too weak for what we needed. Switching from 3kg servos to 20kg servos did the trick. With more powerful servos, we then had an issue of how to ensure the gears could grip strongly enough onto the belt in order to tighten and loosen it. We tried several methods of increasing the belt's friction, including rubber bands, lines of hot glue, and sandpaper. Sandpaper worked the best because it added grip without adding much bulk to the belt profile.

Finding the best sensor to use and the most reliable positioning and attachment scheme was another challenge that we faced. We tried a small 3in flex sensor briefly, but found that the difference in bend created with a tighter belt versus a looser one was not significant enough for the sensor to detect reliably. Therefore we decided to stick with our pressure sensor. We had to devise a way to focus the force onto the sensor, since simply placing the flat sensor against the flat belt did not detect significant force from the belt pressing on the body. We placed a small plastic block on top of the sensor that would rest between the body and the force sensor, thus effectively concentrating the force and giving a more sensitive reading.

Figuring out the software side of our project was another significant challenge. We started with a very simplistic approach: set an upper and lower force threshold based on our testing and observation, and if the force dips below the lower threshold, tighten the belt, and if it goes above the upper threshold, loosen the belt. However we experienced problems where the force would oscillate right around a threshold, causing the belt to continually tighten and loosen back and forth without stopping. To combat this, we kept a variable outside of the main loop that tracked the current state of the belt (tighten, loosen, or idle) and the previous state of the belt. We set a condition that the belt cannot reenter the tightening mode for the first 2 seconds after finishing a previous tighten, and the same for loosening. This prevented oscillation while still allowing for small adjustments.
